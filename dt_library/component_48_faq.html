Essential Travel Information

Namibia has a population of approximately 3.03 million people, which is remarkably small for a country of its physical size. That low population density is one of the reasons travellers often describe Namibia as feeling vast, open, and wonderfully uncrowded.

Namibia covers about 824,290 square kilometres, making it a very large country by global standards. To help picture its scale, it is about 1.5 times larger than France, which explains why distances between destinations can be much longer than many first-time visitors expect.

Namibia is a highly multilingual country. English is the official language, but everyday life includes a wide mix of local and regional languages and dialects. There are more than 20 languages and dialects spoken, making it a culturally rich and continuously fascinating place to explore.

Yes, Namibia is consistently ranked as one of the safest and most politically stable countries in Africa. Violent crime against tourists is extremely rare. However, just like anywhere in the world, basic common-sense precautions are advised, such as not leaving valuables visible in unoccupied vehicles.

The majority of the country is connected by well-maintained gravel roads rather than tar. While these are beautifully scenic, they require careful driving. We highly recommend renting a 4x4 or high-clearance vehicle for comfort and safety, and adhering to the gravel speed limit of 80 km/h.

Namibia is a year-round destination, but the dry winter months from May to October are generally considered the best time for game viewing. During this time, wildlife congregates around permanent water sources, making animals much easier to spot, and the daytime temperatures are incredibly comfortable.

Namibia offers the luxurious scale of untouchable nature. It's not just about standard safari drives; it’s about watching the sun rise over the towering red dunes of Sossusvlei, exploring the desolate shipwreck-strewn Skeleton Coast, and waking up to absolute silence in private wilderness reserves. It defines high-end, uncrowded exploration.
